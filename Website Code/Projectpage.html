Poverty & Redlining

      Redlining was the practice of enacting racially restrictive zoning in cities, prominent most in the
    
  United States and Canada. This involved characterizing various neighborhoods with “residential
    
  security maps” showing which areas were more ‘desirable’ for real estate investment. A letter
    
  grading system with corresponding colors was used to rank neighborhoods.

    
  Areas with an ‘A’ ranking were generally affluent suburbs on the outskirts of cites, highlighted
    
  in bright green on maps. Areas with ‘B’ rankings were “still desirable” and marked in blue on
    
  maps. Areas with ‘C’ rankings were referred to as “declining” and marked in yellow on maps.
    
  Areas with ‘D’ rankings were referred to as “declining” and were marked in bright red on maps;
    
  these were older neighborhoods, most often with majority Black populations. While this also
    
  affected other nonwhites, as well as Jewish and Irish immigrants; Black Americans were
    
  primarily impacted by these policies.

    


    
  These redlining maps were not produced by the FHA (Federal Housing Administration), they
    
  were almost entirely produced by the private sector to meet requirements set by the FHA.
    
  Private lenders, small banks, large federally insured banks, and home insurance companies
    
  would mostly refuse to provide their services or give home loans to people in yellow and
    
  redlined areas.

    
  Home ownership is one of the most effective ways for a family to build generational wealth. It
    
  is no surprise that redlining had a devastating social and economic impact on Black Americans.
    
  South LA is 38% Black, 56.7% Latino, and 2.2% White. Only 36.9% of South LA residents own
    
  their home, while 63.1% rent. Overall, low home ownership rates and high poverty rates persist
    
  to the present day in historically redlined areas across the United States. Present day
    
  gentrification of these areas further increases the cost of living; disproportionally pushing out
    
  low income, nonwhite residents out of a neighborhood that their communities were redlined
    
  into decades before. There is a strong connection between historical redlining and present-day
    
  poverty in these areas, this map helps show that link.

Metrorail & Walkability

    
  These food desert areas in South LA are mostly lower income and have much lower car
    
  ownership rates. In car dependent Los Angeles, it is difficult to get around without owning a
    
  vehicle. Residents of these areas are often limited to stores that they can walk to; there is a lack
    
  of supermarkets in South LA that sell healthier foods or fresh produce. Many residents will walk
    
  to a nearby convenience store or fast-food place instead since they are often the only options
    
  within walking distance. The city of Los Angeles has rapidly expand their rail transit network
    
  over the past decade, and plans to expand it further. Rail lines can provide increased mobility to
    
  underserved communities and could assist in reducing the severity of food deserts if placed
    
  right.

    

    
  The interactive map provided shows the LA metro rail lines with the area of South LA outlined.
    
  The metro rail system crosses into the very outskirts of South LA; it is almost like Los Angeles
    
  did not want people from this community to ride their rail system. The rail circumnavigates
    
  South LA. While it does reach some underserved areas, the rail system mostly connects monied
    
  suburbs and wealthy enclaves to Downtown LA; giving car commuters an alternative to
    
  congested freeways. Taking cars off the road is still a good thing, but the fact remains that there
    
  are many people in LA that don’t own a car at all and we should be taking care of their needs
    
  first.
